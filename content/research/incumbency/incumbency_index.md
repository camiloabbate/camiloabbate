abstract: Is holding office a blessing or a curse for political parties in developing democracies? Standard political economy models assume that incumbency confers structural advantages through resource access and visibility. However, using a Regression Discontinuity Design (RDD) on a panel of Paraguayan municipal elections (2001–2021), we document an interesting paradox: the average incumbency advantage is statistically indistinguishable from zero. This aggregate null result is driven by a deep partisan asymmetry — while the hegemonic Colorado Party (ANR) obtains a positive incumbency dividend, opposition parties suffer a systematic incumbency penalty. We attribute this divergence to organizational structure: while the ANR leverages a unitary party machine to consolidate local power, opposition forces rely on transient electoral coalitions that face high coordination costs, making holding office a liability for non-institutionalized alliances.
